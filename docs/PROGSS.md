PROGSS

The main result of this study is the adaptation of a development of a health and safety
support system in extreme contexts (PROGSS), originally built for ICE environments. The
analytical functioning of the PROGSS system—conceived as a continuously evolving system
rather than a closed solution - is schematically presented in Figure 1. The system was designed to
enable progressive interdisciplinary action, consistent with the complexity inherent to extreme
environments and emergency scenarios. The figure details the input, processing, and output layers,
as well as the types of data mobilized at each stage or phase, highlighting the adaptive logic that
sustains the system throughout the operational cycle and its feedback loops. This architecture
explicitly accounts for the limitations associated with small datasets, which are common in isolated
environments, and for their potential analytical implications. The use of AI techniques is
implemented in a responsible and incremental manner, emphasizing transparency, continuous
human oversight, and the explicit acknowledgment of the system’s operational limits. Machine
learning models and rule-based engines are employed in a complementary fashion for clustering,
risk stratification, and trend prediction, avoiding automated decision-making while providing
assertive decision support rather than substituting specialized human judgment and
interdisciplinary health and safety assessment.

Figure 1. PROGSS scheme (own design).
It is structured based on an interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary architecture that is aimed
at the integration, analysis, and automated response to multiple human and environmental data in
polar missions and analog spaceflight simulations. The results showed that a perceptible reduction
in the time required to manage health and operational tools, mainly due to the integration of all
daily-use resources into a single environment. This reduction was not quantitatively tested but
conceptually observed as a practical gain in efficiency, potentially contributing to the potential for
further development and deployment in future simulations.
For example, during the analog mission, the daily integration of biomedical and
psychological indicators revealed consistent patterns between reduced sleep quality and increased
self-reported stress. When these correlations exceeded predefined limits, the system's rule-based
module generated real-time suggestions for adaptive adjustments to workload or rest periods.
Although rudimentary, programmed in Google Sheets spreadsheets, it was possible to understand
how responses to events were being provided, and how the integration of usual mission tools
facilitated access to them, even considering that not all indicators had been entered into the system

due to limitations in accessing raw data – partially facilitated by the position held by one of the
authors of the paper. The adjustment responses were simplified, only for functionality verification.
The implementation of PROGSS during the analog mission resulted in an integrated
operational platform composed of two functional steps and five mission phases, applicable to both
polar and simulated space environments. In Step A, a contextualized integration platform was
established, allowing interdisciplinary protocols (psychological, physiological, biological, and
medical) to be prepared, standardized, and deployed across analog and digital devices. This
enabled continuous data collection and storage even under limited communication conditions. Step
B operationalized this platform across five temporal phases: pre-mission, early mission, midmission and critical events, end-of-mission, and post-mission. Baseline profiles were generated
before exposure to environmental stressors, followed by continuous individual and group
monitoring throughout the mission and structured follow-ups at one and six months post-mission.
As a result, PROGSS enabled the systematic collection, storage, and analysis of behavioral,
physiological, and psychological indicators, and the delivery of structured feedback to participants,
researchers, and operational staff, supporting mission replicability and comparative analysis across
analog environments.
The system was designed to collect data only once per indicator, eliminating redundancies
between areas and favoring the interoperability of information between technical and scientific
teams. For example, it is not necessary to repeatedly collect basic information across
psychological, medical, or physiological studies. Each indicator - such as motivation level, sleep
quality, or sociodemographic data, as sex, age, etc. - is collected once and then integrated into the
shared system, allowing specialists from different fields to independently cross-analyze the
information. In stages A and B, the system allows significant gains in terms of clarity in
longitudinal monitoring and fluidity in the mission schedule, reducing tasks for scientific studies.
The viability of the system was validated through the execution of the simulation by the
developer herself, presented to participants to express their perception for improvement activities,
as planned by the SSM, with field observations and practical application of the protocols on a
specific route, designed in Google Sheets as a prototype, including the schedule in a daily progress
status for mission participants and supervisors. The results indicate that the model is applicable
both in space simulations and in polar expeditions. The model was tested as a decision-support

system designed to anticipate potential critical events, support operational and clinical decisionmaking, and contribute to crew well-being through early warning and adaptive feedback
mechanisms.. The system also provides mechanisms to check the daily schedule of activities and
tasks, with a progress bar. Layered data collection and storage structure facilitate efficient data
sharing, the addition of the use of hybrid digital and analog instruments, including offline/online
tools, wearables (e.g., smartwatches, eye-tracking devices, movement sensors), and physiological
sensors (e.g., temperature, perspiration) can enhance data accuracy and minimize respondent
burden.
During the analog mission, the analytical layer of PROGSS was used to integrate
physiological signals (e.g., heart rate and sleep quality) with self-reported emotional and
behavioral data, allowing the system to generate dynamic risk profiles for individual crew
members. These profiles made it possible to detect emerging patterns of fatigue, stress, and
reduced performance over time. Based on these combined indicators, the system produced rulebased adaptive suggestions, such as recommendations to adjust workload, introduce additional rest
periods, or activate targeted psychological support routines. Although implemented in a simplified
prototype format, these automated outputs demonstrated how the integrated data streams could be
translated into actionable decision-support information during mission operations. In addition, the
aggregation of individual and group-level data allowed the system to capture collective behavioral
trends, supporting the identification of periods of increased psychosocial risk associated with
confinement and workload dynamics in the analog habitat. Figure 2 illustrates the conceptual
model of the system, visually representing the technology adapted to the context of space analog
missions.
