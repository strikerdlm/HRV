Dear Prof. Trajkovic,

We submit for consideration in *IEEE Transactions on Human-Machine Systems* a regular paper titled above, presenting the Operational Performance Indicator (OPI) framework — a task-calibrated, weighted composite readiness index for aviation and unmanned aircraft system operators, integrating SAFTE biomathematical fatigue, heart-rate-variability autonomic markers, Multiple Resource Theory cognitive-load modifiers, a Warm-type vigilance-decrement model, and a Chen-type teleoperation-latency penalty. Seventeen operator categories — ten manned aviation and seven UAS — receive explicit per-task weight profiles. The framework is distributed under the MIT license as an open-source reference implementation and is demonstrated end-to-end through one illustrative 128-minute heart-rate-variability recording analysed under three task hypotheses.

The contribution sits within the journal's scope of "human systems and human organizational interactions including cognitive ergonomics, system test and evaluation, and human information processing concerns in systems and organizations." Specifically, the paper addresses three research questions: (RQ1) whether task-calibrated weighting changes readiness classification under summary-consistent autonomic state; (RQ2) whether theory-derived per-task weights are identifiable on operationally-realistic operator samples; and (RQ3) whether a linear weighted-composite substrate supports traceable, audit-friendly safety-case logic for human-machine system gate decisions without sacrificing classification fidelity relative to opaque end-to-end classifiers. The present manuscript addresses RQ1 through the worked example and frames RQ2 and RQ3 as the validation roadmap, with field instrumentation planned in a subsequent protocol publication.

Our claims are deliberately bounded. We claim framework definition, software distribution, and an illustrative demonstration. We do not claim validated diagnostic accuracy against operator outcomes, regulatory clearance, or numerical parity with external HRV or fatigue packages. Field calibration of the per-task weights against operator performance is stated as the next research step.

We believe the paper is a fit for THMS for three reasons. First, the framework directly addresses the journal's chartered domain of cognitive ergonomics and system test and evaluation in human-machine systems. Second, the inspectable linear-composite design — auditable line by line, extensible by demand analysis rather than retraining — supports the safety-traceable engineering posture that distinguishes IEEE THMS from neighbouring venues. Third, the Warm-type vigilance and Chen-type teleoperation-latency components have no direct analogue in prior composite readiness frameworks and align with active topical interest in human-machine teaming and operator-loop design within the SMC community.
